INTERNATIONALISATION OF RUPEE BY RBI:

A SIMPLE GUIDE

India’s external sector has been showing exceptional progress by integrating itself with the global economy over the last three decades. It is also a preferred destination for foreign investors due to its transparent and liberal policies concerning FDI (Foreign Direct Investment).

With the ongoing global crisis, owing to several factors such as the global COVID-19 pandemic and geopolitical conflicts like the Russia- Ukraine war, the world economy has been plummeting significantly. Due to the continued global financial crisis, the US dollar has weakened resulting in a significant appreciation in the value of the rupee.

With the Indian economy growing at a rate of 8% per annum, the value of the Indian currency is mainly determined by the market. Along with a large number of foreign exchange reserves in the Asian region, the Indian rupee is a worthy candidate for internationalisation.

What does internationalisation of the rupee mean?

A process that involves the use of local currency in foreign transactions is referred to as the internationalisation of rupee. It is referred to as the use of the Indian rupee for import, export and other current account transactions. The act of internationalisation can also be described as adopting full capital account convertibility. This means that there is no restriction on the amount of rupees that can be converted into foreign currency to buy an asset abroad.

Why is India internationalising its rupee?

India is internationalising its rupee due to the following reasons:

  • Use of the rupee in cross-border transactions helps reduce the risk for Indian businesses.
  • The need to hold on to foreign exchange reserves reduces.
  • To reduce excessive dependence on foreign currency
  • Internationalising the rupee will help in enhancing the Indian economy
    • Process of internationalising the rupee

    Even though invoicing exports and imports in rupee was long permitted, it was used only for limited purposes. RBI enabled rupee settlement for external trade in its July 2022 scheme which helped create a more specific framework for internationalising the Indian rupee.

    The country has also opened up the facility to make external commercial borrowings in rupees. This is done through ‘masala’ bonds. They are defined as bonds that are dominated by rupee and are issued outside India by Indian entities. These bonds were launched to promote Indian culture on a global scale. The first masala bond was issued by the International Finance Corporation for Indian Infrastructure projects in 2014.

    Advantages of rupee internationalisation

    The following are the advantages of internationalisation of the Indian rupee:

    1. It provides Indian businesses with a chance to grow globally
    2. Reduces cost of foreign exchange transactions
    3. Rupee internationalisation helps reduce the price volatility associated with dollars
    4. It helps improve India’s standing as a global power
    5. Increases the demand for rupee in the world economy
    6. It gives ease of access to capital and helps lower the borrowing cost
    7. Provides greater integration of the Indian economy with the rest of the world
    Disadvantages of rupee internationalisation

    The following are some of the risks associated with internationalising the Indian rupee:

    1. India might be subject to an external shock due to the large independence given to residents and non-residents for fund flow from within and outside the country and from one currency to another.
    2. Short - term funds could be withdrawn by non-residents.
    3. It would make the country’s monetary policy challenging as it would need to be revised.
    Final thoughts

    Internationalising rupee has risks which require the country to formulate strong macroeconomic policies. Policies thus formulated need to fulfill stability and liquidity. These are the important goals that a policy formulated in India needs to achieve. Once these norms are fulfilled, it would enable the Indian rupee to be accepted as an international currency.

    Out of all the emerging markets in the economy, the Indian rupee is a possible contender for the status of international currency as the Indian economy is progressing faster. It is also due to the country’s ability to withstand the negative effects of the global economic slowdown.

    If implemented effectively, internationalising the Indian rupee can help the country achieve its growth prospects soon.
